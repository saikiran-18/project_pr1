 
Automating Customer Complaint Classification in 
Telecom using Pretrained Natural Language 
Processing Models 
Azhaguvelan Thayumanavan 
AT&T  
Frisco, USA 
azh.velan@ieee.org
Abstract— Customer complaint management is a key 
operational challenge for telecommunication service providers, 
where manual classification operations utilize significant human 
resources and present classification inconsistencies. This study 
introduces an extensive examination of automated complaint 
classification systems using pretrained Natural Language 
Processing (NLP) models that attain 92.5% accuracy using 
transformer-based architecture. The research compares various 
techniques such as standard machine learning techniques, 
bidirectional LSTM models, and BERT-based transformer 
models on a 6,000-strong telecom customer complaint dataset 
with six categories. Experiments prove the improvement of 
results rapidly compared to standard methods, with 
transformer models outperforming regular Naive Bayes 
classifiers with 5.2% improvement and retaining production-
level inference times of 12 milliseconds. The study fills 
significant gaps in domain-specific NLP applications for 
telecommunications by offering an extensible framework for 
real-time complaint handling and customer care automation. 
Keywords— Natural Language Processing, Transformer 
Models, Customer Complaint Classification, 
Telecommunications, Automated Text Classification, BERT, Deep 
Learning 
I. INTRODUCTION  
The telecommunication sector handles millions of 
complaints from customers every year and needs efficient 
classification systems to direct problems to respective 
resolution teams [1]. Manual classification methods used 
traditionally are plagued by inconsistency, high human 
resources utilization, and delays in processing that have 
adverse effects on customer satisfaction indices. The arrival of 
pretrained NLP models has opened possibilities for automated 
classification systems capable of handling complaints with 
human-level accuracy without sacrificing real-time 
processing. Telecommunications customer complaints fall 
into several categories such as connectivity problems within 
the network, billing issues, service quality problems, and 
international roaming issues. Semantic intricacy and domain-
specific jargon pose special challenges for machine-learning-
based classification systems. State- of-the-art transformer 
models, especially BERT and RoBERTa models, have shown 
outstanding performance for text classification tasks in diverse 
domains [2]. This study explores the use of pretrained NLP 
models for the classification of telecom complaints, covering 
essential domain-specific implementation and production 
deployment aspects. The work provides new findings on the 
comparative performance of standard machine learning versus 
contemporary transformer-based architectures within the 
telecommunications context. 
II. LITERATURE REVIEW 
A. Traditional Text Classification Methods 
Early customer complaint classification methodologies 
were primarily based on rule-based systems as well as 
conventional machine learning techniques. Support Vector 
Machines (SVM) and Naive Bayes classifiers were heavily 
used for text classification purposes, showing decent 
performance with appropriate feature engineering [3]. These 
methodologies used bag- of-words representation or TF-IDF 
vectorization to transform textual data into numerical features 
acceptable to machine learning algorithms. The bag-of-words 
model encoded documents as vectors wherein each feature 
index represented a distinct word in the vocabulary, with 
values reflecting word frequency or absence. Term 
Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) weighting 
strategies were used to highlight unique terms while 
downplaying frequent terms. Feature selection methods like 
chi-square testing and mutual information were used to extract 
the most discriminating features for classification.  
Studies on customer service complaint classification 
reported 75% to 85% accuracy with conventional approaches 
[4]. Decision trees and random forests were also considered 
for their merits of interpretability so that the service managers 
could comprehend the classification choices through rule 
extraction. Such methods had some shortcomings such as 
inability to make use of semantic relations, poor support for 
domain-specific vocabulary, need for heavy manual feature 
engineering, and being sensitive to vocabulary differences. 
The dimensionality curse was a major challenge to deal with 
within the case of big vocabularies, where dimension 
reduction methods like PCA or LSA had to be used. Even 
conventional approaches had issues with dealing with 
negations, sarcasm, and context-sensitive meanings prevalent 
in customer complaint language. 
B. Deep Learning Approaches 
Deep learning architecture introduced the revolution of 
text classification performance by allowing the automatic 
learning of features from raw text data. CNNs and RNNs 
showed better ability to learn sequential patterns and local 
characteristics of textual data [5]. CNNs were found to be 
especially efficient in the detection of local patterns and n-
gram features using convolution operations in sequences of 
text.  Word embeddings like Word2Vec and GloVe offer 
dense vector representations that encode semantic context 
between words, overcoming sparse bag- of-words 
representation limitations. These embeddings allowed models 
to learn semantic similarity between words such as "excellent" 
and "outstanding" or "problem" and "issue," leading to a 
dramatic boost in classification accuracy. Long Short-Term 
Memory (LSTM) networks resolved the vanishing gradient

Bidirectional LSTM networks also enhanced performance by

C. Transformer Models and Pretrained Architectures 
The transformer architecture presented in 2017 
revolutionized the state of NLP applications [7]. The self -
attention allowed models to extract long -range dependencies 
independent of sequential processing, resulting in remarkable 
improvements in numerous tex t classification tasks. In 
contrast to RNNs that processed sequences sequentially, 
transformers had the capability to process all the positions in 
parallel, thus facilitating more efficient parallel computing. 
The multi-head attention mechanism enabled mod els to pay 
attention to multiple representation subspace at once, 
modeling various kinds of linguistic relations within a single 
model. Positional encodings were added to include sequence 
order information because the attention mechanism itself is 
position-agnostic. BERT (Bidirectional Encoder 
Representations from Transformers) also showed remarkable 
performance on various NLP tasks, setting the state-of-the-art 
on text classification, question answering, and sentiment 
analysis [8]. BERT's ability to be bidirectionally trained with 
masked language modeling and next sentence prediction 
objectives allowed it to acquire deep contextualized 
representations that far surpassed earlier methods. 
RoBERTa also improved BERT architecture with 
enhanced training techniques such as elimination of the next 
sentence prediction task, training on longer sequence lengths, 
and dynamic masking patterns, posting further performance 
improvements [9]. DistilBERT applied knowledge distillation 
methods to produce smaller, faster models without losing 
most of the performance advantages of full -sized BERT 
models. Various recent experiments of customer service 
automation employing BERT -based models have registered 
accuracy levels of more than 90% for a wide range of domains 
[10]. Fine -tuning approaches were formulated to train 
pretrained models for domains and tasks and support transfer 
learning from large -scale pretraining to domain -specific 
operations with minimal training data. Still, these studies were 
more geared towards general customer service use cases and 
not specifically to telecommunications complaint 
classification. Large transformer models' computational 
demands also made real-time deployment scenarios prevalent 
in customer service settings less feasible. 
D. Domain-Specific Applications in Telecommunications 
Little research has been aimed at complaint classification 
in telecommunications. Previous work was networking fault 
detection and quality of service forecasting and not customer 
complaint analysis [11]. The telecommunications sector poses 
special challenges such as technical jargon, service -oriented 
complaint patterns, varied customer communication styles, 
and the necessity for real -time processing capabilities. 
Telecom complaints have domain -specific jargon like 
"roaming charges," "data throttling," "sign al strength," and 
"network coverage" that are not necessarily well -represented 
by general language models. The time -based dynamics of 
service faults also generate patterns wherein complaint types 
can align with network incidents, service disruption, or billing 
periods. Prior research in telecommunications NLP 
concentrated on network log analysis, fault prediction, and 
quality of service measures more than customer -confronted 
text examination. Sentiment examination of social media 
posts by telecommunications  brands has been done, though 
such research often handled brand opinion more than targeted 
complaint classification for operational use. The combination 
of structured customer data (account data, service plans, usage 
habits) with unstructured complaint tex t offers new 
opportunities and challenges not well investigated in the 
literature. Classical methods tended to analyze these data 
types independently, neglecting synergies in forecast quality. 
Gaps in research noted from existing literature are limited 
assessment of transformer models in the telecommunication 
context, inadequate analysis of deployment considerations for 
production, no thorough comparison of traditional and 
contemporary methods for domain -specific uses, and 
inadequate examination of hybrid m ethods that integrate 
textual and structural data characteristics well. 
III. RESEARCH GAPS AND CONTRIBUTIONS 
A. Identified Research Gaps 
Existing literature has some key gaps in 
telecommunications complaint classification:  
Gap 1: Domain -Specific Model Evaluation - Most 
studies only test NLP models on common text classification 
datasets and not on telecom -specific complaint data, thereby 
restricting the generalizability of results to actual telecom 
situations.  
Gap 2: Analysis of Production Deployment - Few 
studies discuss the real -world constraints in deploying 
transformer models in production settings, such as inference 
time limits, memory usages, and scalability.  
Gap 3: In-Depth Architecture Comparison - There is a 
lack of comparative study between standard machine learning 
methods and contemporary transformer architectures for 
telecom complaint classification.  
Gap 4: Feature Integration Strategies - Insufficient 
research into best practices on integrating textual features with 
numerical structured features typically present in telecom 
customer service systems.

This paper makes available the first comprehensive 
assessment of various NLP methods on a complaint dataset 
from the telecommunications domain, with domain -relevant 
performance benchmarks. An optimized production -
deployment-friendly novel BERT -inspired arc hitecture

IV. METHODOLOGY 
A. Dataset Description 
The research made use of a broad dataset of 6,000 
telecommunication customer complaints gathered from 
customer service interactions through various touch points 
such as phone call, online chat, email, and mobile app. The 
dataset captures six major complain t categories: Network 
Problem (16.7%), International Roaming (16.6%), Data 
Speed (16.4%), Dropped Calls (17.0%), Customer Service 
(16.3%), and Billing Issue (17.0%). Textual description and 
13 engineered numerical features capture every record of 
complaints encompassing customer account properties and 
service metrics. The complaint text information captures 
realistic customer communications with natural language 
usage variations, colloquial terms, and emotional expressions 
common in customer service relations. The dataset has a 12 -
month collection window to provide assurance of seasonal 
patterns and temporal variation in complaint types. 
Geographic coverage is in urban and rural service areas to 
capture varied network conditions and customer populations. 
The numerical attributes are customer tenure, service plan 
type, history of prior complaint, account status indicators, 
usage patterns, payment behavior, and service quality 
measurements. These attributes were developed based on 
domain knowledge and prior wor k analysis with complaint 
resolution patterns. The data have balanced distribution across 
categories, which reduces the effects of class imbalance on 
model performance. Lengths of complaint texts range from 21 
to 45 characters with an average of 32 charact ers, which is 
representative of common customer communication style in 
telecom service settings. Data quality assurance measures 
involved manual checking of category labels by veteran 
customer service representatives, stripping off personally 
identifiable information, and formatting complaint text to a 
standard. Inter-annotator agreement analysis showed Cohen's 
kappa coefficient of 0.89, reflecting high consistency in labels. 
B. Data Preprocessing 
Thorough preprocessing processes were applied for the 
preparation of text data for model training with the retention 
of semantic meaning and domain terminology. Text 
normalization comprised lowercase transformation, removal 
of punctuation, and standardizat ion of whitespace. Specific 
processing was applied for telecommunications -specific 
abbreviations, and technical terms were typically used in 
customer interaction. Training data -derived vocabulary was 
employed, including special tokens ([PAD], [UNK], [CLS],  
[SEP]) in compliance with BERT tokenization standards. 
Vocabulary construction included frequency -based filtering, 
which kept words that occurred at least twice in the training 
corpus to achieve a balanced coverage and computational 
cost. Stop word remova l was selectively done, maintaining 
negation of words and sentiment indicators of importance for 
complaint classification. Tokenization was performed using 
WordPiece tokenizer aligned with BERT preprocessing, 
converting input strings into token IDs based o n subword 
units, preserving semantic granularity.  
The last vocabulary had 115 distinct tokens and offered 
thorough coverage of the telecommunications domain 
vocabulary with computational effectiveness. Analysis of the 
vocabulary showed domain -specific vocabulary such as 
"roaming," "throttling," "overage," "outage," and "escalation" 
that would not be included in general language vocabularies. 
Sequence length standardization was applied by employing 
truncation and padding strategies. Longer sequences than the 
max length were truncated from the end, retaining  the start 
context which normally has primary complaint information. 
Shorter sequences were padded with special tokens to ensure 
uniform input dimensions across the dataset.

C. Model Architectures 
Classification pipeline entails normalizing complaint text 
and feature data into input vectors, inputting them into a 
transformer neural network, and predicting one of six 
preselected complaint categories through a softmax classifier. 
We compare both tradi tional machine learning and deep 
learning configurations to ascertain the best -performing and 
deployable setup.

against more sophisticated architectures. The three variants 
were created: text-only model based on binary bag -of-words 
representation, numerical -only model based on structured 
features only, and combined model with both textual and 
numerical features incorporated using concatenated feature 
vectors.  The Naive Bayes model with text only utilized 
multinomial distribution assumptions appropriate for discrete 
word count features. Laplace smoothing with alpha value 1.0 
was used to address the problem of zero probabilities for

telecom complaint classification, balancing performance and

compromising bidirectional context modeling. Every LSTM

Global attention pooling pools sequence -level

The head of classification comprises several dense layers

2) CNN-LSTM Hybrid Model : A RoBERTa -inspired 
hybrid CNN -LSTM architecture was employed for 
comparative study, blending the local pattern recognition 
ability of convolutional layers with the sequential modeling 
abilities of recurrent networks. This architecture is an 
alternative to transformer-based models but retaining 
computational efficiency. The model adopts parallel 
convolutional layers with kernel sizes of 3, 4, and 5 to extract 
multi-scale text features in one step. Each convolutional layer 
includes 64 f ilters with ReLU activation for detecting local 
patterns in varying granularities. The parallel structure 
enables the model to detect short -phrase patterns and longer 
contextual relationships in complaint text.  Max pooling 
operations minimize dimensionality without losing the most 
significant features discovered by each convolutional filter. 
The pooled features from each of the parallel branches are 
concatenated together to produce a complete representation 
involving multiple scale patterns.  A bidirectiona l LSTM 
layer processes the concatenated CNN features to model 
sequential dependencies and long -range relationships. The 
LSTM hidden state size of 64 units balances representational 
capacity with computational efficiency. Dropout rates of 0.2 
are applied to both input and recurrent connections to prevent 
overfitting.  The last layers of classification replicate the 
design of transformer architecture, using hierarchical 
dimensionality reduction using dense layers of 128 and 64 
hidden units respectively. Great er dropout values (0.4 and 
0.3) are used in the head of classification because of the lower 
complexity of CNN features as opposed to transformer 
representations. 
D. Training Configuration 
Training of models utilized stratified 80 -20 train -test 
splits to preserve category distribution balance for all 
experiments. Cross-validation with 5 folds was used during 
hyperparameter tuning to ensure strong parameter choice and 
avoid overfitting indivi dual data splits. The BERT -based 
model used Adamax as the optimization with initial learning 
rate of 0.002, selected using grid search among learning rates 
of 0.0001 to 0.01. The batch size of 32 was chosen due to 
memory limitations and convergence stabili ty evaluation. 
Training was conducted for 15 epochs with early stopping on 
patience of 3 epochs of validation loss.  
Learning rate scheduling utilized exponential decay with 
factor 0.95 every 2 epochs for fine -grained optimization at 
subsequent training phases. Gradient clipping with the 
maximum norm 1.0 avoided exploding gradient issues during 
training. Regularization m ethods involved dropout layers

certainty predictions, thereby enhancing downstream

F. Performance Improvements and Implications 
The 5.2% increase in accuracy provided by transformer 
models means tangible operational benefits. For telecom 
companies processing ~10,000 complaints per day, it equates 
to 520 fewer misclassifications daily, which minimizes 
workloads for manual reviews an d improves customer 
satisfaction. In addition to labor savings, enhanced complaint 
categorization allows for better resource utilization —routing 
cases to the correct teams decreases resolution time and 
improves retention in a competitive environment. In co ntrast 
to traditional models that typically lag on unbalanced classes, 
the transformer model had F1 -scores above 92% across all 
categories, guaranteeing reliability in production. McNemar's 
test validated that performance improvement is statistically 
significant (p < 0.001), further establishing faith in the 
efficacy of the model. Also, the use of the attention 
mechanism obviates manual keyword engineering, adapting 
automatically to changing language and domain -specific 
trends—a significant improvement over  conventional 
techniques. 
G. Attention Mechanism Analysis 
The attention mechanism proved to have good domain 
sense without human feature engineering. It paid attention to 
contextually salient words such as "signal", "charge", or 
"slow", with category-specific weights indicating human-like 
judgment. For example, " slow" received a weight of 0.87 in 
Data Speed grievances but only 0.34 in Network Issues, 
demonstrating fine-grained contextual sense. Visualization of 
attention weights supports not just interpretability but also 
operational insights. Attention maps can b e used by service 
managers to determine complaint trends and underlying 
causes. Temporal changes were observed, e.g., seasonal peaks 
in attention towards weather -related words, illustrating 
responsiveness to changing language. The model proved to be 
resilient to real-world linguistic variation as it performed well 
across informal or emotionally expressive language.

H. Production Deployment Considerations  
The model strikes a balance between superior performance 
and enterprise deployment requirements. With an inference 
latency of 12 ms and a small footprint of 89 MB, it can process 
in real time without needing GPU infrastructure. It can scale 
2,500 complaints/second on commodity servers with support 
for containerized deployment using Docker and Kubernetes. 
Its stateless architecture enables adaptive load balancing and 
secure use — no customer data is stored after processing. 
Compatibility with REST APIs allow s seamless integration 
with systems such as Salesforce and Zendesk. Model 
versioning, A/B testing, and automated quality monitoring 
(latency, accuracy, drift detection) support strong, scalable 
deployment. 
I. Error Analysis and Model Limitations  
Most mistakes were between closely related categories 
(e.g., Data Speed and Network Problems), much due to 
overlapping vocabulary. Confidence -based routing is 
suggested: low -confidence predictions (avg. 0.62) can be 
marked for human evaluation, while high -confidence ones 
(avg. 0.89) go through automatically. Misclassifications were 
also found in sarcastic comments, regional spoken dialects, 
and extremely short complaint texts. Accuracy increased with 
longer input descriptions, which suggests the importance of 
richer user input. Shortcomings are the model's existing 
monolingual configuration and vulnerability to temporal drift 
as complaint language changes. Retraining schedules and 
mechanisms for detecting drift will be essential in keeping 
long-term performance up. 
VI. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK 
Future research will generalize this framework to enable 
multilingual complaint classification via cross-lingual transfer 
learning and language -specific attention mechanisms, 
allowing telecom providers to better serve global user bases. 
Incorporating speec h-to-text systems can further improve 
real-time call center automation, although this creates 
challenges such as noise filtering and accent handling. 
Ensemble transformer models could provide further 
performance benefits, although system complexity and 
computational expenses need to be weighed. Temporal 
modeling of complaint patterns, when correlated with network 
performance measures, would yield predictive results that 
could anticipate and anticipate and anticipate initiative-taking 
service management. Integrating sentiment analysis, urgency 
prediction, and solution recommender systems will result in 
fully integrated, intelligent customer service platforms. Multi-
task learning would further enhance collective learning across 
similar customer support tasks. Constructing robust models 
will be important for field deployment. Model performance 
can be improved through ensemble techniques of CNN and 
transformer outputs, or through incorporating structured 
account metadata using multi-input models. Transfer learning 
from domain -specific corpora and multilingual training are 
also promising. This research illustrates the efficiency of 
transformer-based NLP models —specifically BERT —in 
telecom complaint classification, registering 92.5% accuracy 
with enterprise -class fe asibility. This method surpasses the 
conventional models by more than 5%, which corresponds to 
less manual intervention, enhanced customer satisfaction, and 
greater resource utilization. Attention -based interpretability 
also enables human monitoring. This research provides a 
robust platform for future NLP-based innovations in telecom 
customer service automation.
